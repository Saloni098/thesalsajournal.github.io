-Saloni Dwivedi

16 July 2021

This book, (deep sighs), doesn't take me to some other world. Well, if you ask me, it kept me wandering here itself. This world. Our very own world. We live here. We die here. Sometimes we fear death. Sometimes, we fear oblivion. But this book beautifully fills in the fact that oblivion is not something to be feared. Death is inevitable. We'll all have to leave this place someday. It is all about the choices which we make around.

Life is never a wish granting factory. But for some reasons, which we don't know, we do meet some people in our lives who turn out to be true blessings. We feel home in them. Love creeps in and we don't even realize it.

I fell in love, like you fall asleep : slowly and then all at once

And after that we don't fall more. We just look upon that soul forever.

Hazel Grace, a 16 year old protagonist of the story, is a cancer patient. Got some mets in her lungs when she was 13. She considers herself to be pretty unextraordinary. She has a strong interest in reading and considers depression is not the side effect of cancer. In fact depression is a side effect of dying.

But, in fact, depression is not a side effect of cancer. Depression is a side effect of dying. (Cancer is also a side effect of dying. Almost everything is, really.)

This book has beautifully drawn the outlines of metaphors in our life. There are a lot of things which drew me closer to her character. The thing that she doesn't write poetry but has a keen interest in reading it. And for me, this is the one. The way she reads poetry and doesn't have a hand on writing it. Silently reminding me of how important are the ones who are there to listen to us without saying a word. Just listening. Nothing else. And this is the kindest thing someone can do to us. The way she knows the things and still does not point them out just to listen to them. And I feel this is humble.

Also that, pain and sufferings do not have say in our happiness.

"This is an old argument in the field of Thinking About Suffering, and its stupidity and lack of sophistication could be plumbed for centuries, but suffice it to say that the existence of broccoli does not in any way affect the taste of chocolate"

Thoughts on 'THE FAULT IN OUR STARS'.
